Not all sandwiches are created equal - Is the humble sandwich a climate change culprit?

In the first study of its kind, “Understanding the impact on climate change of convenience food: Carbon footprint of sandwiches”, Professor Adsida Azapagic and her colleagues at the University of Manchester, carried out and in-depth study of various sandwiches throughout their life cycles.

To better understand the environmental impact of all these sarnies, the Manchester team looked at over 40 different sandwich types, recipes, and combinations as well as how they are made, packaged, transported, and stored. In addition, they considered the waste produced in making them, as well as the stale, rotten, or simply outdated sandwiches that are thrown away.

According to the British Sandwich Association (BSA) 11.5 billion sandwiches are eaten in the UK each year. About half of those are made at home and the other half are bought over the counter in shops, supermarkets and service stations around the country. The UK spends nearly £8bn a year on sandwiches, at an average cost of £2, but the cost to us all could be far greater - 9.5 million tonnes of carbon dioxide, the equivalent annual carbon emissions of 8.6 million cars in Britain alone.

But don’t be hasty and ditch this much-loved staple of the British diet, as they likely have a lower environmental impact that a full restaurant meal.

By just by changing recipes and ditching packaging waste could result in a 50% drop in sandwich-related carbon emissions.
